I begin this post with a cautious heart, as it contains personal religious reflections. "If you hold a different view, you are undoubtedly correct. Any shortcomings in this perspective are entirely my own, so I ask for your kind understanding."  


I am Catholic, and my baptismal name is Anselmo. On February 27th, I completed my first 54-day Novena of the year. I’m leaving these thoughts behind to capture the many reflections that filled my mind during this journey. For some reason, this particular prayer period felt exceptionally long and full of distractions. While a "Novena" typically suggests nine days, this specific devotion is a 54-day journey—27 days of petition followed by 27 days of thanksgiving. It requires a steadfast heart, offered every single day without fail. 


I deeply respect all faiths. Whether it be Catholicism, Buddhism, Won Buddhism, Protestantism, or Islam, I believe every sincere belief system carries positive values that deserve respect. Whenever I speak with atheists about religion, I am reminded of a certain anecdote (the exact source escapes me, but it is a story I cherish).  


**A Dialogue Between an Atheist and an Old Monk**

 **Atheist:** "Brother, what if you open your eyes after death and find that neither the God nor the Heaven you devoted your life to exists? Wouldn't you feel cheated, having lived a life of self-denial for nothing?"

**Monk:** "Not at all. Even if God does not exist, because I believed in Him, I was able to spend my life loving and forgiving others, enjoying a profound peace of mind. For that reason alone, my life has been sufficiently meaningful and happy. I have no regrets."


This conversation resonates with 'Pascal’s Wager.' While the mathematician Blaise Pascal argued that believing in God is the most logical "bet" in terms of probability, the old monk’s answer is far warmer and more positive than pure logic. (It is also worth noting that the genius mathematician John von Neumann returned to Catholicism in his final days.)  


I am also reminded of a witty comment by a certain entertainer:  


"The reason Jesus and Buddha remain so popular is that they have remained 'silent' for thousands of years. If Jesus had climbed down from the cross to complain about his pain, or if Buddha had nagged us about our career choices, no one would seek them out today."  


It was a lighthearted joke, but it touched me deeply. The very act of praying in silence and leaning our hearts toward the Divine provides us with immense comfort and positive energy.
In conclusion, it is because of my faith that I can strive to be humbler and a better version of myself. The fact that I can willingly offer a prayer for someone else is, in itself, reason enough for my faith. Until the day I close my eyes for the last time, I wish to live in deep gratitude to the God in whom I believe.
